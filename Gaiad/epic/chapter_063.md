# Chapter 63: The {{c|Children}} of {{c|Two}} {{c|Worlds}}

From {{c|Tetrus}}, father of the land's great nations,
Two sons arose who chose two different roads:
One broke in full from water's old foundations,
One heard the lake still calling through the woods.

The first would father those who sealed their birth
In eggs of air and broke from water's hold,
Who built their children's lives across the earth
Where even drought could not diminish bold.

But {{c|Amphibos}} still heard the water calling
And chose the threshold as his sacred home,
Where forest met the flood-plain, night-mist falling,
And coal-swamp vapors rose like silver foam.

"I am the child of two great worlds," he cried,
"The river's son who breathes the open air,
I walk the land yet keep the lake my bride
And find my truest home where both are there."

For {{c|Amphibos}} had learned the ancient art
Of transformation deep within his kind:
His children entered life with larva's heart
Then shed that skin to leave the streams behind.

They burst from water, limbs still trembling new,
And breathed the air of coal-swamp forests tall,
Where {{c|Lepidos}} spread canopies of blue
And {{c|Meganeura}} ruled the humid hall.

Three children claimed the kingdoms of his care:
Great {{c|Temna}}, {{c|Salamandrus}}, {{c|Caecila}}—
The warrior, the wanderer, the rare
Blind burrower beneath the ancient floor.

Great {{c|Temna}} was his eldest, warrior-born,
Who built her empire in the darkest fen,
She ruled the swamp-edge, midnight until morn—
The greatest beast that walked the coal swamp then.

Great {{c|Temna}} stretched her body flat and wide,
Her skull a shield of bone both broad and strong,
She lay in shallow waters, still in pride,
And let the coal-swamp currents bear her long.

Her children thrived where oxygen ran high
And grew to sizes none had witnessed yet,
With flat-boned heads and cold, unblinking eye—
The {{c|temnospondyls}} ruled without regret.

Her greatest son stretched nine great lengths of man
And ruled the rivers like a living lord,
None challenged him throughout his fearsome span—
His jaws spread wide beneath each bog and ford.

From {{c|Temna}} came a dynasty of might:
Young {{c|Eryops}} walked upon the solid shore
And hunted under both the day and night,
A barrel-chested warrior built for war.

Her daughter {{c|Mastod}} ruled the river's breast,
Whose skull was flat as any table laid,
She swam in silence, letting current rest
Her patient body in the water's shade.

Great {{c|Branchios}} chose to never leave the stream—
He kept his gills throughout his ancient days
And breathed through feathered crimson plumes that gleam
Like scarlet flowers in the coal-swamp haze.

Yet {{c|Temna}}'s empire could not last the age—
The world grew dry and forests turned to bone,
And {{c|Temna}}'s children left their heritage
In ancient fossil beds of rock and stone.

Young {{c|Salamandrus}} chose a quieter path—
Four limbs and long tail trailing through the moss,
He found the places sheltered from the wrath
Of sun, and counted moisture as his cross.

He taught his children patience in the cold,
To seek the shaded stone and mountain brook,
To move but slowly, cautious, shy, and bold,
And give the rushing world a second look.

From {{c|Salamandrus}} came the pale {{c|Proteid}}
Who never grew beyond his larval state,
Who breathed through gills throughout his life indeed
And found within the cave his own true fate.

And {{c|Salamandrus}}' daughters sought the cold
And swallowed gulps of air when first they woke,
They breathed through skin when winter's grip took hold
And drew them deep beneath the river's cloak.

Last {{c|Caecila}} descended underground
And shed her eyes like garments worn too thin,
In worm-rich tropical earth she always found
More riches than the sighted found within.

Her skin grew rings like earthworms, smooth and tight,
And many who encountered her would say
She was no more than worm—but in the night
She proved herself a hunter in her way.

Her children found the dark continents vast
Where moisture kept the tropical soils rich,
And {{c|Caecila}}'s line, though others thought it past,
Found perfect home in every fertile niche.

Then came the drying of the ancient world—
The {{c|Permian}} winds swept cold across the land,
The coal-swamp forests fell and were unfurled
As desert swallowed what the swamp had spanned.

Great {{c|Temna}}'s empire shrank as lakes grew small,
The giant temnospondyls lost their thrones,
And those who needed much heard ruin's call
And left their grandeur written into bones.

Yet {{c|Amphibos}} had taught his children well
The art of living lightly on the land:
When times grew hard and all familiar fell,
The small survived through patience, modest hand.

For those who need not much may always find
The hidden moisture in the driest stone,
The shaded pool, the leaf-pressed earth behind,
The damp beneath a log, the cave, the bone.

From ruin came renewal—three great kinds
Emerged refined and new from ruin's test,
Each bearing what the ancient lineage finds
When life strips back its armor to the best.

Fair {{c|Rana}} leapt to claim the marsh's grace—
Her young were born in water, pure and whole,
As tadpoles first who earned their limbs by race,
As if each life must mirror life's own soul.

She sang: "My children die and are reborn!
They live as fish, then shed their tails for land—
What greater proof that life should not be mourned
But celebrated, shaped by nature's hand?"

Ten thousand kinds of frogs arose and knew
The ancient secret of the transformation's rite,
Their singing voices rang through evening dew
And called the summer rainstorm through the night.

And {{c|Salamandrus}} passed his grace along
To children patient, slow of breath and pace,
Who asked not much of life but still grew strong
And found their kingdom in the shaded place.

While {{c|Caecila}}'s great-grandchildren pressed on
Through every continent that moisture knew,
Beneath the soil where even light was gone
They made a kingdom in the dark and dew.

Thus from {{c|Amphibos}}' ancient, blessed line
Came frog and toad and salamander bright,
Came caecilians through tropical earth and vine—
The children of both water's depth and light.

They teach us of the threshold's sacred truth:
That life is sometimes strongest at the seams—
The child who lives in water in its youth
Then sheds that form for air and wider dreams

Is keeper of a double-gifted art
Who holds two worlds within one trembling frame,
Who carries water's coolness in a heart
That beats to land's more fierce and fiery flame.

So honor those who master life's great edge,
Who need not choose between the deep and high,
Who build their homes upon the water's ledge
And breathe as freely in the stream as sky.

From {{c|Amphibos}} through {{c|Temna}}'s ancient reign,
Through {{c|Rana}}'s song and {{c|Salamandrus}}' grace,
Through {{c|Caecila}}'s long winding underground plain—
The children of two worlds still hold their place.
