The Realm of Oita is a realm in Japan.

Right — modern Ōita Prefecture corresponds roughly to the old provinces of Bungo and Buzen in northeastern Kyushu. Here’s a detailed breakdown:

⸻

1. Old Provinces
	•	Bungo Province (central and southern Ōita)
	•	Covered most of the Ōita Plain and southern coastal areas.
	•	Fertile plains allowed early wet-rice agriculture.
	•	Historical castles and temple sites suggest a moderate population density relative to Kyushu’s mountainous interior.
	•	Buzen Province (northern Ōita + northeastern Fukuoka)
	•	Extended into what is now Fukuoka Prefecture; northern Kyushu coast along the Suo Sea.
	•	Strategic position along the Seto Inland Sea and routes to Honshu.
	•	Historically a frontier zone in some periods, but also a trade corridor.

⸻

2. Historical Importance
	•	Trade and transport: Ōita’s location along the Bungo Channel connected Kyushu to Honshu and Shikoku; coastal settlements were hubs for commerce.
	•	Feudal history: During the Sengoku and Edo periods, Ōita’s clans controlled strategic ports and fertile lands. Hita (in Bungo) became a notable castle town under local lords.
	•	Cultural sites:
	•	Ancient shrines and Buddhist temples date back to Heian and Kamakura periods.
	•	Evidence of Kofun-era burial mounds in some areas.

⸻

3. Population Patterns
	•	The Bungo Plain supported denser settlements, while mountainous areas remained sparsely populated.
	•	Overall, Ōita was moderately populated, with pockets of cultural and administrative significance but smaller in scale compared to Yamato or Kyushu’s northern regions like Chikuzen/Bizen.

⸻

In short: Ōita Prefecture = Bungo + Buzen, a region of fertile plains, strategic coastal routes, and moderate historical population, with ancient cultural and archaeological sites reflecting early Kyushu settlement.
